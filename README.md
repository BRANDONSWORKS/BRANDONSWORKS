  Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID) is a mental health condition characterized by the presence of two or more distinct personality states or identities within a person. These identities may have unique behaviors, memories, and perceptions, often forming to cope with severe trauma. DID develops as a protective response to overwhelming childhood stress, typically chronic physical, emotional, or sexual abuse before the age of 7-9 years, when the personality is still forming. During traumatic experiences, the mind dissociates, creating separate identities to compartmentalize pain or memories. This dissociation helps the child survive extreme situations by mentally escaping them. Over time, these fragmented parts may become distinct identities, each managing different aspects of life. DID is considered a childhood disorder because it originates from trauma during critical developmental stages, before a cohesive sense of self is established. Early brain plasticity and the lack of a fully integrated personality increase vulnerability to dissociation. It is not caused by genetics but by repeated external trauma. The disorder often remains unrecognized until adulthood when stress or life events trigger symptoms. DID is different from day-to-day dissociation, as it involves significant disruption to identity and memory. Diagnosis requires careful evaluation by a trained professional, and it is often misunderstood or stigmatized. Treatment typically involves therapy to integrate identities and process underlying trauma, fostering a cohesive self.

In Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID), a system refers to the collection of distinct identities, also known as alters, within a single individual. These alters are parts of the person's mind that have developed separate roles, perspectives, and often their own memories, to help the person cope with traumatic experiences. The system as a whole represents the way these parts interact, communicate, and coexist internally.
